**A single adult should plan roughly $250–560 per month for groceries in 2026 depending on how frugally they shop, and a family of four $975–1,900 — those are the USDA's official food-plan ranges. As a share of income, 10–15% of take-home pay is the practical benchmark.** Here's how the tiers break down and how to find the number that's right for *your* situation.

## The USDA benchmarks for 2026

The [USDA publishes four food plans](https://www.fna.usda.gov/research/cnpp/usda-food-plans/cost-food-monthly-reports) at rising cost levels, updated monthly for inflation:

| Plan | Single adult / month | Family of four / month |
|------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Thrifty | ~$247–309 | ~$975–995 |
| Low-Cost | ~$323–371 | ~$1,150–1,250 |
| Moderate | ~$392–465 | ~$1,450 |
| Liberal | ~$499–566 | ~$1,900+ |

The national average lands around **$363 per person per month**. Exact figures vary by age and sex (the USDA tables are granular), but the tiers are the useful part: they tell you what disciplined, normal, and comfortable grocery spending look like at 2026 prices.

## The income rule: 10–15% of take-home pay

Dollar benchmarks ignore your income; percentages don't. Groceries fit inside the "needs" half of the [50/30/20 rule](/blog/what-is-the-50-30-20-rule) — and 10–15% of take-home pay is the range where most sustainable budgets settle. On a $4,000/month take-home, that's $400–600. Run your own numbers in the [50/30/20 calculator](/tools/50-30-20-budget-calculator).

## Why your grocery number feels higher than these

Three usual reasons. First, "groceries" quietly absorbs non-food: household goods, pharmacy items, pet food — often 15–20% of a supermarket receipt. Second, food delivery and coffee runs live in a different mental category but the same wallet — that's [discretionary spending](/blog/what-is-discretionary-spending) wearing a grocery costume. Third, nobody actually adds it up: purchases are spread across taps, cards, and cash, so the monthly total stays a guess. ## How to actually lower it (without misery)

The USDA Thrifty plan proves ~$250–310/person is possible, but grinding to the minimum rarely lasts. What does: a per-week cap instead of per-month (four small deadlines beat one big one — see [spending limits](/blog/what-is-a-spending-limit)), a list-first shop, and tracking the category live so you see "groceries: $310 of $400" *before* the next trip, not after. ## FAQ

### How much should one person spend on groceries per month?

By USDA 2026 benchmarks: ~$250–310 on a thrifty plan, ~$390–465 moderate, ~$500–560 liberal. The national average is about $363/person. As an income share, aim for 10–15% of take-home pay.

### How much should a family of four budget for groceries?

Roughly $975–995/month on the USDA Thrifty plan, ~$1,450 on Moderate, and $1,900+ on Liberal at 2026 prices.

### Is $200 a month enough for groceries for one person?

It's below the USDA Thrifty benchmark (~$247–309), so it requires deliberate effort — meal planning, minimal waste, discount stores. Possible, but leave slack for price swings.

### What percentage of income should go to food?

Around 10–15% of take-home pay for groceries; add another few percent if you eat out regularly. If food exceeds ~20%, it's usually delivery and restaurants, not the supermarket — separate the two categories and the fix becomes obvious.
