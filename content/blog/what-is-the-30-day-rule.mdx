The **30-day rule** is a simple saving strategy: when you feel the urge to make a non-essential purchase, you wait 30 days before buying. If you still want the item after the waiting period and it fits your budget, you buy it. If the urge has faded, you keep the money. It turns impulse buys into deliberate decisions.

## How it works

The rule works by inserting a delay between wanting and buying. Say you spot a 200-dollar jacket you didn't plan for. Instead of checking out, you write it on a wishlist with the date and price, then set a reminder for 30 days out. During that month, the emotional spark that drove the urge usually cools. When the reminder arrives, you make a calm choice with a clear head.

A quick way to run it:

1. **Pause** the moment you feel the urge, and leave the item in the cart or on a list.
2. **Write it down** with the price and today's date.
3. **Wait 30 days**, moving the cash you would have spent into savings.
4. **Revisit** and decide. Buy only if you still want it and it fits your plan.

## Why it matters

Most impulse purchases are powered by short-lived emotion, and emotion fades faster than you think. The 30-day rule uses that fade to your advantage, filtering out the buys you'd have regretted while still letting the genuinely worthwhile ones through. It also builds a savings habit almost by accident, because the money you set aside during the wait often just stays saved.

Common mistakes: using it for true essentials (it's meant for wants, not needs), or skipping the write-down step, which makes it easy to "forget" the pause and buy anyway. For smaller purchases, a shorter version, like a 48-hour rule, keeps the friction proportional.
