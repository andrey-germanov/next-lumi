**By 25, a solid position is a starter emergency fund of $1,000–$3,000 plus the habit of saving 10–15% of every paycheck; roughly half your annual salary in total savings puts you ahead of schedule for the classic "1x salary by 30" benchmark.** The median American under 35 has just $5,400 in the bank — so if you have anything saved at 25, you're not behind. Here's the honest breakdown.

## What the numbers say at 25

There's no official benchmark for age 25 — [Fidelity's guideline](https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/retirement/retirement-guidelines) starts at 30 (1x your salary). Working backwards, being on pace at 25 looks like:

| Milestone | Target at 25 |
|-----------|--------------|
| Emergency fund | $1,000 starter; 1 month of expenses is great |
| Total savings | ~0.3–0.5x annual salary = on pace for 1x by 30 |
| Retirement accounts | Contributing *anything*, ideally with full employer match |
| High-interest debt | A payoff plan in motion ([debt calculator](/tools/debt-payoff-calculator)) |

For context: the median household under 35 holds [$5,400 in savings and about $18,880 in retirement accounts](/blog/average-savings-by-age). Half of your peers are below that.

## Why the habit beats the number at 25

At 25 the dollars are small but the compounding runway is enormous — $200/month started at 25 beats $400/month started at 35 by retirement (run it in the [compound interest calculator](/tools/compound-interest-calculator)). The number that actually predicts your savings at 35 isn't your balance today; it's whether saving is automatic. Two mechanics do the work: an automatic transfer on payday ([pay yourself first](/blog/what-is-pay-yourself-first)), and knowing where your money goes — because at a 20s income, [lifestyle creep](/blog/what-is-lifestyle-creep) and [forgotten subscriptions](/blog/how-to-find-forgotten-subscriptions) can quietly eat the entire 15%.

## The 25-year-old catch-up plan (if you have $0 saved)

Start with visibility, not sacrifice. Track one full month of spending — every coffee, every tap. This is where most plans die of friction, so remove the friction: say ["lunch twelve dollars"](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing) and it's logged, let [Apple Pay purchases record themselves](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending). [Lumi](/) does both free. Month one usually surfaces $100–200 of spending you didn't know about; that becomes your first automatic transfer. Then split your income with the [50/30/20 calculator](/tools/50-30-20-budget-calculator) and put the first savings toward a [$1,000 starter emergency fund](/blog/what-is-an-emergency-fund).

## FAQ

**Is $10,000 saved at 25 good?** Yes — that's roughly double the under-35 median and, at a typical mid-20s salary, on pace for the 1x-by-30 benchmark. See [how much you need by 30](/blog/how-much-savings-by-30).

**Should I save or pay off student loans first?** Build the $1,000 starter fund first, capture any employer 401(k) match (it's a 50–100% instant return), then attack debt above ~6–7% interest before saving more.

**How much of my paycheck should I save at 25?** 10–15% including retirement is the standard target; if that's unrealistic right now, [start smaller but automatic](/blog/how-to-save-money-on-a-low-income) — the habit scales with your income.
