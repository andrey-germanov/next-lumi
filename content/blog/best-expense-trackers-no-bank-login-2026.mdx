Most budgeting apps open with the same demand: connect your bank account. To work, they ask for your online banking login, then route it through a third-party aggregator like Plaid so the app can read every transaction, balance, and account you own.

A lot of people quietly refuse, and they have good reasons. Handing your banking credentials to a middleman means one more company holding the keys to your financial life, one more database that can be breached, and one more party profiling where you shop and what you earn. Some banks even warn that sharing login details with third parties can affect fraud protection. Add everyone who distrusts automated feeds after watching an app miscategorize months of spending, and you have a large, underserved audience that wants to track money without linking a single account.

The good news: an **expense tracker with no bank login** can be just as useful, and often more mindful. Manual entry, receipt scanning, and Apple Pay imports keep your data under your control while still giving you clarity on where the money goes. This guide covers the best no-bank-login trackers in 2026, what each does well, and how to pick one.

## The best expense trackers with no bank login

### Lumi

**Best for:** iPhone users who want the fastest possible logging without ever linking a bank.

[Lumi](/) is built around a single idea: if logging an expense takes more than a couple of seconds, you will stop doing it. It is **iOS-only**, and it leans hard into what the iPhone does well. You can log a purchase in about two seconds using **Back Tap** — a double or triple tap on the back of your phone that opens a quick-entry screen — or let Lumi **auto-import Apple Pay** transactions so tap-to-pay purchases show up ready to categorize. There is no bank login anywhere in the flow, and your data stays on your device.

Beyond speed, Lumi adds the conveniences that make manual tracking sustainable. **AI receipt scanning** pulls the total, merchant, and date from a photo. **Voice input** lets you say what you spent instead of typing. An **AI month-end forecast** looks at your pace so far and projects where you will land, so overspending is something you catch mid-month rather than discover too late. It supports **150-plus currencies** and **category budgets**, which makes it a genuine option for travelers and expats as well as everyday budgeters.

**Pricing:** Free plan with the core logging features. **Premium is 4.99 dollars per month** and lifts limits on AI scans, budgets, and analytics.

**Pros:**
- Two-second logging via Back Tap or Apple Pay import
- AI receipt scan, voice input, and an AI end-of-month forecast
- No bank login; data lives on device
- 150-plus currencies and category budgets

**Cons:**
- iOS only — no Android version
- No bank sync, so recurring or forgotten charges must be captured by you

### Finny

**Best for:** People who want AI-assisted entry and automatic Apple Pay capture.

Finny is a privacy-first tracker that never asks for bank credentials and works offline, syncing when a connection is available. Its standout feature is **Tap to Track**, which captures Apple Pay purchases using Shortcuts the moment they happen. It also accepts flexible AI input: type a natural-language line like "groceries 42.50," speak it, or scan receipts, including batching several photos at once. Finny supports over 150 currencies and keeps each transaction in its original currency while converting your totals.

**Pricing:** A free tier covers unlimited manual tracking, custom categories, and charts. Paid plans start at roughly **1.99 dollars per month** (about 17.99 dollars per year), which is inexpensive relative to most competitors.

**Pros:** AI and voice input, Apple Pay tap-to-track, strong multi-currency handling, low paid price.
**Cons:** Feature set overlaps heavily with other AI-first trackers, so the right pick often comes down to platform and interface preference.

### Goodbudget

**Best for:** Couples and anyone who budgets with the envelope method.

Goodbudget is a digital take on the classic **envelope system**: you divide your income into named envelopes (rent, groceries, fun) and spend only what each holds. It is proudly manual by design — you enter transactions yourself or import a CSV your bank exports — and it syncs envelopes across devices so partners can share a budget in real time.

**Pricing:** The **free plan** includes a fixed number of envelopes, one account, and two devices with a year of history. **Premium runs about 8 dollars per month when billed annually** (around 80 dollars a year) and unlocks unlimited envelopes, more devices, and longer history. Automatic bank sync is available on Premium but entirely optional, so you can stay fully manual on either tier.

**Pros:** Excellent for proactive, plan-ahead budgeting; great for shared finances; manual by default.
**Cons:** The free plan's envelope and device caps feel tight; the interface is more spreadsheet than delightful.

### PocketGuard (manual mode)

**Best for:** People who want the option of automation later but prefer to start manual.

PocketGuard is best known for its "In My Pocket" number — how much is safe to spend after bills and goals. While it is often marketed around bank connections, you can run it **without linking anything** by creating a cash account, setting your balance, and logging transactions as you make them. That keeps your credentials private while still giving you PocketGuard's spending-limit view.

**Pricing:** A **free plan** covers a limited number of accounts. **PocketGuard Plus is about 12.99 dollars per month** (with cheaper annual and lifetime options) and adds unlimited accounts, custom categories, and debt-payoff tools.

**Pros:** Clear "safe to spend" framing; manual accounts are fully supported; scales up if you ever change your mind on sync.
**Cons:** Manual mode means updating balances yourself; the paid tier is pricier than several manual-only rivals.

### Wally

**Best for:** Receipt-driven tracking across many currencies.

Wally centers on capturing spending as it happens. You can **scan and upload bills and receipts**, log expenses manually, and track across a very wide range of currencies, which makes it popular with frequent travelers. It layers charts, recurring-bill reminders, and category budgets on top. Bank syncing exists in some versions but is not required — manual and receipt entry stand on their own.

**Pricing:** Free to download, with the usual caveat that some advanced features sit behind a paid tier. Confirm the current in-app plans before committing, as Wally has shipped several app variants.
**Pros:** Strong receipt capture, broad currency support, useful reminders.
**Cons:** The lineup of Wally apps can be confusing; check which version you are installing and what its manual features include.

### Fudget

**Best for:** People who find every other app too complicated.

Fudget throws out categories, graphs, and sync-by-default in favor of one thing: a **running balance**. You add income and expenses as simple line items and instantly see what is left. There are no bank connections at all — it is manual by nature — and it runs on iPhone, Android, Mac, and Windows.

**Pricing:** **Fudget Basic is free** and covers monthly and weekly planning, manual entry, passcode or Face ID lock, and multiple currencies. **Fudget Plus** (a yearly subscription) adds cross-device sync, unlimited entries, CSV export, reminders, and themes.
**Pros:** Genuinely simple; nothing to learn; works everywhere.
**Cons:** No categories or analytics on the basic experience, so it is planning, not deep insight.

### Spendee

**Best for:** Shared wallets and people who like visual spending breakdowns.

Spendee pairs manual entry with clean pie and line charts, and lets you create **shared wallets** so a household or trip group can pool a budget. On the free plan you enter transactions yourself, choose categories, add descriptions, and attach photos. Bank sync and extra wallets are paid perks — never a requirement — so a no-login setup is straightforward.

**Pricing:** Free tier for manual tracking; paid plans (with a short free trial) add bank sync, more wallets, and sharing. Entry pricing is modest.
**Pros:** Attractive visuals, shared wallets, optional-only automation.
**Cons:** The nicest collaboration features are paywalled; heavy manual users may hit free-tier limits.

### 1Money

**Best for:** Android users who want fast, icon-based manual logging for free.

1Money is a clean, ad-free tracker built around **large category icons** — tap a category, enter an amount, done. It supports multiple currencies with automatic exchange rates, budgets, charts, and debt or savings tracking, and it currently does **not offer bank sync**, so everything is manual. The catch is platform: 1Money is **Android-only**, so iPhone users should look elsewhere (Lumi or Finny fit that gap).

**Pricing:** Free, with no ads — unusual for a no-cost app.
**Pros:** Fast entry, no ads, no bank login, solid free feature set.
**Cons:** Android only; manual entry means you carry the discipline.

## How to choose

There is no single best **expense tracker with no bank login** — the right one depends on how you spend and what you value. A few questions to narrow it down:

- **What phone do you carry?** On iPhone, Lumi and Finny use Apple-specific tricks (Back Tap, Apple Pay import, Shortcuts) to make logging nearly effortless. On Android, 1Money and Fudget are strong free choices.
- **How do you want to enter data?** If you hate typing, prioritize **receipt scanning, voice input, or Apple Pay import** (Lumi, Finny, Wally). If you like a deliberate, plan-first ritual, envelopes (Goodbudget) or a running balance (Fudget) suit you better.
- **Solo or shared?** Couples and households should look at Goodbudget or Spendee for real-time shared budgets.
- **How much structure do you want?** Fudget is minimalism; PocketGuard and Lumi add forecasting and limits; Goodbudget enforces a full envelope system.
- **Do you travel?** Multi-currency support (Lumi, Finny, Wally, 1Money) matters if you spend abroad.

Whatever you pick, the habit is what makes it work. A tracker you actually open every day beats a feature-rich one you abandon. ## FAQ

**Are expense trackers without bank login safe?**
They are generally the more private option, because there is no shared banking credential and no aggregator holding a live feed of your accounts. Apps like Lumi keep data on your device rather than pulling it from your bank. As always, use a device passcode and any in-app lock the tracker offers.

**Is manual tracking really worth the effort?**
For many people it is more effective, not less. Typing or scanning each purchase forces a moment of awareness that automated feeds skip, and features like Back Tap, voice input, and receipt scanning cut the effort to seconds. The trade-off is that you, not a bank feed, are responsible for capturing every transaction.

**Can I track spending on an iPhone without connecting a bank?**
Yes. On iOS, Lumi logs an expense in about two seconds via Back Tap and can auto-import Apple Pay purchases, all with no bank login and data kept on device. Finny offers a similar Apple Pay tap-to-track approach. Both work fully without linking an account.
