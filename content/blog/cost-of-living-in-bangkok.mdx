**A single expat in Bangkok spends roughly 35,500–59,000 THB per month (about $1,000–1,700) in 2026 — a decent one-bedroom near transit, street food mixed with restaurants, air conditioning, and a social life included.** Bangkok remains one of the best cost-to-quality ratios of any major city. Here's where the money actually goes.

## Bangkok monthly budget at a glance

| Category | Monthly cost (THB) | ~USD |
|----------|--------------------|------|
| 1-bedroom condo, decent area near BTS/MRT | 15,000–25,000 | $420–700 |
| Studio near BTS Ari / MRT Phra Ram 9 | 12,000–18,000 | $340–500 |
| Food (street lunches + some nice dinners) | 8,000–15,000 | $225–420 |
| Transport (BTS/MRT + occasional Grab) | 2,500–4,000 | $70–110 |
| Utilities + internet (AC is the big one) | 3,000–5,000 | $85–140 |
| Entertainment, gym, misc | 5,000–10,000 | $140–280 |
| **Realistic total** | **35,500–59,000** | **~$1,000–1,700** |

Sources: [Numbeo Bangkok](https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/in/Bangkok), [Expatistan](https://www.expatistan.com/cost-of-living/bangkok) — mid-2026 figures.

## Rent: transit distance is the price

Bangkok rent is priced in minutes-to-BTS, not square meters. The same 15,000 THB gets a tired studio on Sukhumvit or a bright one-bedroom fifteen minutes' walk from the station. The classic expat mistake is paying 25,000+ for a tower next to Asok and then wondering where the budget went — while On Nut, Ari, or Phra Ram 9 deliver the same life at 60% of the price.

## Food: the two-economy city

Bangkok runs two parallel food economies: a 60 THB street pad krapow and a 350 THB mall lunch, three minutes apart. Which economy you default to *is* your food budget — 8,000 THB months and 15,000 THB months feel surprisingly similar day to day. This is precisely the spending that vanishes from memory: dozens of small cash and QR payments a week. [Tracking cash spending](/blog/how-to-track-cash-spending) is where Bangkok budgets are won, and voice logging ("pad thai sixty baht") takes two seconds at the stall.

## The AC bill and other honest surprises

Electricity is the utility that hurts: running air conditioning daily adds 2,800–3,200 THB/month, and Bangkok's heat makes it non-negotiable most of the year. Visa runs, health insurance, and the occasional flight home are the other lines first-year expats forget — build them in as [sinking funds](/blog/what-is-a-sinking-fund) rather than surprises.

## FAQ

### How much money do you need per month in Bangkok?

35,500–59,000 THB (~$1,000–1,700) for a comfortable single-expat life in 2026. Survival-mode budgets under 30,000 THB are possible; most people don't enjoy them for long.

### Is Bangkok cheap for expats in 2026?

Relative to any Western capital, decisively yes — though prices have risen since 2023. The cost-to-quality ratio (transit, food, healthcare) remains among the world's best.

### How much is rent in Bangkok for foreigners?

12,000–18,000 THB for a good studio near transit, 15,000–25,000 for a one-bedroom in a decent area. Distance to BTS/MRT moves price more than size does.

### Can you live in Bangkok on $1,000 a month?

Yes — at the bottom of the comfortable range: studio slightly off-transit, street-food-first eating, BTS over Grab. Set [spending limits](/blog/what-is-a-spending-limit) on the mall-and-delivery category and it works.
