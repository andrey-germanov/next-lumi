## The Poblado tax

Medellín's rent map is a gradient of the same apartment at three prices: $700–1,200 in El Poblado's towers, $500–800 in Laureles' walkable grid, $300–700 fifteen minutes out in Envigado or Sabaneta. Poblado charges for proximity to the nomad scene, not better living — Laureles has become the connoisseur's pick precisely because it's the same life minus the markup. Metro access matters more than the postcode: Colombia's only urban rail makes car-free living genuinely easy.

## Daily costs: honest pesos

Groceries at $100–200 (Éxito, D1, and the plaza minorista doing the heavy lifting), $5 almuerzos, $25 fiber internet, world-class healthcare at $60–120 — Medellín's baseline is disarmingly cheap. The leak is the gringo-pricing gradient: rooftop brunches, imported groceries, and Poblado nightlife run 2–3x local rates, and [lifestyle creep](/blog/what-is-lifestyle-creep) happens in dollars while you think in pesos. Six-digit peso amounts blur fast ($1 ≈ 4,000 COP); a [category limit](/blog/what-is-a-spending-limit) on nightlife keeps the drift visible.

## FAQ

### How much money do you need to live in Medellín?

$1,000–1,600/month for comfortable single living in 2026; $1,500 is the honest floor for a polished solo setup, and lean budgets work from $900.

### Is Medellín still cheap in 2026?

Outside El Poblado's international bubble, decisively yes — rent, food, transport, and healthcare cost a fraction of North American equivalents. Poblado prices have crept toward mid-tier US urban levels.

### How much is rent in Medellín?

$700–1,200 furnished in El Poblado, $500–800 in Laureles, $300–700 in Envigado/Sabaneta. Laureles is the value-quality sweet spot for most.

### What about safety and hidden costs?

Prepagada health cover ($60–120) is the non-negotiable line most budgets should carry. Otherwise the hidden cost is the dollar-priced expat circuit — track it as its own category and Medellín stays the bargain it promises.
