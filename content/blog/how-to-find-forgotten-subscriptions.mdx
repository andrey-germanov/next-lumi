Somewhere right now, a service you don't remember signing up for is preparing to charge you. Maybe it's the meditation app from a New Year's resolution, the cloud storage from a phone you no longer own, or a free trial that quietly became a paid plan in 2024. Forgotten subscriptions are pure waste — you get zero value by definition — and finding them is one of the highest-hourly-rate financial tasks that exists. Here's the 30-minute hunt.

## The six hiding places, in order of yield

### 1. Apple subscriptions

Settings → your name → **Subscriptions**. This one list covers everything ever billed through your Apple ID: apps, iCloud, Apple services, and — crucially — **expired trials with renewal dates still pending**. Check the "inactive" section too; it tells you what you already escaped and what patterns you repeat.

### 2. Google Play subscriptions

In the Play Store: profile picture → **Payments & subscriptions** → Subscriptions. If you've ever owned an Android device or used Google services, charges can live here even while you're a full-time iPhone person now.

### 3. PayPal automatic payments

PayPal is where subscriptions go to be forgotten, because charges show up on your statement as just "PayPal." Log in → Settings → **Payments** → Automatic payments. People routinely find software, VPNs, and donation plans from years ago still active here.

### 4. Card statements, three months back

Export the last 90 days for every card — including the old card you keep "for emergencies" — and scan for repeating merchants. Watch for the disguises: an unfamiliar company name (billing entities often differ from brand names), amounts that repeat but at odd intervals, and small charges under €5 that your eye skips past.

### 5. Email receipts

Search your inbox for "renewal", "receipt", "your subscription", and "payment confirmation." This is the only reliable way to surface **annual** subscriptions, which appear on statements once a year and hide for the other eleven months. Sort by sender and you'll get an instant census of everyone who thinks you're their customer.

### 6. The app graveyard

Scroll your own phone. Any app you haven't opened in months that has an account attached is a suspect — delete-the-app is not cancel-the-subscription, and app deletion is exactly how subscriptions become orphans in the first place.

## Found one? Cancel like this

Cancel through the platform, not the app: Apple and Google cancellations take effect immediately and reliably, while in-app "cancel" flows are often designed as retention mazes. For direct subscriptions, the cancellation email is your proof — keep it. And if a service offers a steep "please stay" discount, remember that a subscription you don't use is a bad deal at any price.

## FAQ

### Why don't I see the subscription on my bank statement?

Three usual reasons: it bills through an intermediary (PayPal, Apple, Google) so the merchant name never appears; it's annual and last charged months ago; or it's on a card you rarely check. That's why the hunt covers platforms and email, not just statements.

### Do free trials really convert without warning?

Apple now requires notification before a trial converts, and reputable services email you — but the notice arrives in the exact channels people ignore. Treat the trial signup itself as the decision point: log it with its renewal date the day you start it.

### Is it worth checking if I "basically know" my subscriptions?

The consistent research finding is that people underestimate subscription spending by roughly half. If your confident guess is €40/month, the audit typically finds €70–90. Thirty minutes against a few hundred euros a year is the best trade in personal finance.
