**TapSheet and Lumi answer different questions: TapSheet builds you a savings plan from a questionnaire and shows one number — what's left to spend — while Lumi captures what you actually spend with voice, Apple Pay auto-import, and receipt scanning.** The bigger practical difference is the paywall: in TapSheet, even adding transactions requires a subscription after the 3-day trial; Lumi's core logging is free.

## Quick comparison

| | Lumi | TapSheet |
|---|------|----------|
| Core idea | Capture-first: log everything effortlessly, budget from real data | Plan-first: income − fixed expenses − savings = remaining to spend |
| Entry methods | Voice, Apple Pay auto-import, receipt scan, manual | Manual, subscription-gated |
| Free tier | Voice input, Apple Pay import, 10 AI scans/mo, 2 budgets | 3-day trial; transactions require a paid plan |
| AI | Speech parsing, receipt vision, month-end forecast | None |
| Bank login | Never | No |

| Price | Free / $4.99/mo ($39.99/yr) | Subscription (monthly or yearly) required for core use |
| App Store rating | — | ~4.6 (small review base; launched Jan 2026) |

## Where TapSheet wins

The onboarding insight is real: most people don't want fifty categories, they want one number — "how much can I still spend this month?" TapSheet's questionnaire gets a beginner from zero to that number in minutes, and the single-figure display is genuinely calming. If you've never budgeted and want to be told the answer, it's an appealing front door.

## Where Lumi wins

A "remaining to spend" number is only as honest as the expenses behind it — and TapSheet relies on you manually typing every purchase into a subscription-locked form. Lumi gets the same answer from data that collects itself: say ["coffee five euros"](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing), let [Apple Pay taps import automatically](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending), scan receipts. The [AI forecast](/blog/how-to-stop-overspending) then projects your month-end balance from three months of real spending — the same "what's left" answer, computed instead of estimated. And the free tier is a real product, not a trial.

## Which one should you pick?

Complete beginner who wants a guided plan and doesn't mind paying before trying properly: TapSheet's approach may click. Anyone who's ever abandoned a tracker because manual entry got old: Lumi — the capture methods are the difference between week two and month twelve. For the plan itself, the [50/30/20 calculator](/tools/50-30-20-budget-calculator) builds the same needs/wants/savings split for free.

## FAQ

### Is TapSheet free?

Effectively no — there's a 3-day trial, after which adding transactions requires a monthly or yearly subscription. This is the most common complaint in its reviews.

### What is TapSheet's method?

Income minus fixed expenses minus savings goal equals "remaining to spend" — a simplified zero-based approach showing one headline number. The same logic is described in [what is zero-based budgeting](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting).

### Does Lumi show a "safe to spend" number like TapSheet?

Lumi's AI forecast goes further: it projects your month-end balance from your actual 3-month spending rate (excluding outliers) with a confidence score — so the number reflects your real behavior, not just your plan.

### Which is better for a first budgeting app?

If you'll genuinely type in every purchase, either works. Realistically, retention decides it: logging that takes seconds survives; forms behind a paywall usually don't. See [how to track spending so it sticks](/blog/how-to-track-spending-that-actually-sticks).
