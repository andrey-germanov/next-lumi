A shared budget is a spending plan that two or more people create and follow together — most often a couple, sometimes roommates or family members. It answers three questions: what expenses do we share, how much does each person contribute, and where does the shared money actually go each month.

## What a shared budget is not

It is not necessarily a joint bank account, and it is not full visibility into each other's spending. Those are implementation choices. Plenty of working shared budgets run on completely separate accounts, with each person contributing an agreed amount to shared costs and keeping the rest private. The budget is the agreement; the accounts are just plumbing.

## The three models

**Fully joint** — all income lands in one pot, all spending comes out of it, one budget governs everything. Maximum simplicity, minimum privacy.

**Fully separate** — each person keeps their own accounts and tracker; shared costs get split by an agreed ratio and settled monthly. Maximum autonomy, needs light bookkeeping.

**Hybrid** — a shared pot funds rent, groceries, and utilities; personal money stays personal. The most common modern setup, because it makes the split automatic without merging everything.

For the split itself, couples with different incomes usually choose proportional over 50/50 — each contributes the same *percentage* of income rather than the same amount. Our [couple expense split calculator](/tools/couple-expense-split-calculator) shows both options side by side.

## What makes shared budgets fail

One word: friction. If logging a purchase takes a form with five fields, one partner stops doing it within weeks, and the shared picture becomes fiction. The fix is making capture near-instant — voice input ("groceries forty euros"), automatic Apple Pay import — so both partners' numbers stay true without discipline. ## FAQ

### Do we need a joint account for a shared budget?

No. A shared budget is the agreement about who pays what; it works with joint accounts, separate accounts, or a hybrid shared pot. Choose the account structure that matches how much financial privacy each of you wants.

### How do we split shared expenses fairly?

The two standard options are 50/50 (simple, fair with similar incomes) and income-proportional (each pays the same percentage of their income — fairer when incomes differ). Agree on the ratio explicitly and revisit it when incomes change.

### What should be in a shared budget?

Housing, utilities, groceries, household supplies, shared subscriptions, and joint goals like travel or an emergency fund. Personal subscriptions, hobbies, and gifts typically stay outside it — a short shared list survives contact with real life better than a long one.
