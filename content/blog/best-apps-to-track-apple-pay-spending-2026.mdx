Apple Pay is now the default way millions of people pay — a quick tap at the terminal and you are done. But when you open the Wallet app later to see where your money went, you hit a wall. Wallet shows a short, recent list of transactions per card, no spending categories, no budgets, and no way to see a monthly total. For Apple Card it is a little better, yet still far from a real tracker. If you want to actually understand and control your Apple Pay spending, you need a dedicated app.

This guide breaks down how Apple Pay tracking really works, then compares the best apps to track Apple Pay spending in 2026 — including one honest look at each option's trade-offs.

## How Apple Pay tracking actually works

Before you pick an app, it helps to understand what Apple exposes and what it does not.

**Apple Wallet shows only a limited transaction list.** For a typical credit or debit card added to Wallet, you see just your most recent transactions on that device, and how far back you can scroll depends on what your card issuer shares with Apple. Some banks pass along only a handful of recent purchases. There are no categories, no monthly totals, and no budgets — Wallet is a payment surface, not a tracker.

**Apple Card is the exception.** If you use Apple Card, Wallet gives you a richer view: weekly and monthly spending summaries, merchant names, and color-coded spending categories powered by Apple. It is the closest thing Apple offers to real tracking, but it only covers Apple Card purchases — not the other cards you tap with Apple Pay.

**iOS Shortcuts can automate logging.** This is the key that unlocks true Apple Pay tracking. The Shortcuts app has a Wallet automation trigger (labeled "Transaction" on older iOS versions) that fires the moment you tap to pay with a chosen card. You can set it to "Run Immediately" so it logs silently, then hand the transaction off to an action — a spreadsheet, or a tracking app that provides its own Shortcut action. One important limit: this trigger fires for in-store tap-to-pay purchases, not for online Apple Pay checkouts, so app and web purchases still need a manual entry.

That combination — Wallet's automation trigger plus an app that receives it — is what lets you capture spending automatically without ever connecting your bank.

## Lumi — Apple Pay auto-import with no bank login

Lumi (iOS) is built around one idea: logging an expense should take zero effort. Its Apple Pay auto-import uses a one-time iOS Shortcut you set up once. After that, the expense is logged the moment you tap to pay — no typing, no opening the app, no confirmation tap. Because it rides on the Wallet automation trigger, it works entirely on your device and never asks for a bank login.

Lumi does not stop at Apple Pay. For cash, online purchases, or anything the tap automation misses, you have three fast fallbacks: **Back Tap** logging in about two seconds by tapping the back of your phone, **voice** entry where you just say what you spent, and **AI receipt scan** that reads a photo of a receipt and fills in the details. That coverage matters, because Apple Pay's automation only catches in-store taps.

Beyond capture, Lumi supports 150+ currencies with automatic conversion (useful for travelers and expats), and includes an AI forecast that projects where your spending is heading. It offers a free plan, with Premium at $4.99/mo for the advanced features.

**Pros:** Truly automatic Apple Pay logging, multiple no-typing fallbacks for everything else, no bank connection, strong multi-currency support, generous free plan.

**Cons:** iOS only. Like every Shortcut-based tool, the tap automation covers in-store payments, so online purchases rely on the (fast) manual fallbacks.

**Best for:** Anyone who wants their Apple Pay spending tracked automatically without handing over bank credentials. Start with [Lumi](/).

## Finny (Tap to Track) — automatic in-store capture

Finny's Tap to Track feature works on the same principle: an iOS Shortcut listens for your Apple Pay tap and turns it into a categorized expense using AI. A purchase at a grocery store lands in Groceries, a coffee shop lands in Coffee, and you do not categorize manually. Setup takes a couple of minutes, and like Lumi it never connects to your bank — the data comes from the on-device automation, not a bank API.

Finny's pricing is aggressive: core tracking is free, and Finny Pro is $1.99/mo for AI features.

**Pros:** Automatic in-store capture, AI categorization, no bank login, low-cost Pro tier.

**Cons:** The tap automation is limited to in-store tap-to-pay, so online Apple Pay purchases do not trigger it. iOS only.

**Best for:** People who want a low-cost, automation-first tracker focused on everyday in-store spending.

## Copilot — best for Apple Card users

Copilot takes a different route. Instead of the Shortcuts automation, it integrates directly with Apple Card, Apple Cash, and Apple Savings, importing that data from Apple Wallet into the app. Once linked, you see Apple Card transactions in near real time alongside your other accounts, with automatic categorization and polished budgeting and net-worth views.

The catch is scope and price. Copilot's Apple integration covers your Apple Card, Cash, and Savings — not every card you might tap with Apple Pay. And Copilot costs around $95/year with no permanent free tier after the trial.

**Pros:** Deep Apple Card, Cash, and Savings integration; excellent design; broad budgeting features.

**Cons:** Premium price, no free tier, and the Apple integration is centered on Apple Card rather than all Apple Pay taps.

**Best for:** Apple Card holders who want a full-featured budgeting app and do not mind paying for it.

## MoneyCoach — Shortcuts into a structured app

MoneyCoach also leans on the iOS Shortcuts Wallet trigger to log Apple Pay purchases, sending each tap into the app where you get budgets, reports, and spending breakdowns. It is a good middle ground if you want the Shortcut automation but prefer a more traditional, feature-heavy money manager around it. It offers a free version with a paid upgrade for advanced features.

**Pros:** Combines Apple Pay automation with detailed budgeting and reporting tools.

**Cons:** More setup and more features to learn than a capture-first app; the same in-store-only limit applies to the tap automation.

**Best for:** People who want structured budgeting on top of automated Apple Pay logging.

## DIY Shortcuts + Numbers — free and fully custom

If you do not want another app at all, you can build the automation yourself. In the Shortcuts app, create a new automation, choose the Wallet (Transaction) trigger, select the cards you want to track, set it to Run Immediately, and add an action that appends the amount and merchant to a Numbers spreadsheet. On recent iOS versions, Apple Intelligence can even help categorize the entry.

**Pros:** Completely free, private, and endlessly customizable.

**Cons:** You maintain it yourself, there is no built-in budgeting, categories, or forecasting, and spreadsheets get tedious fast. Online purchases still are not captured.

**Best for:** Tinkerers who enjoy building their own system and only need a running log.

## FAQ

**Can I track Apple Pay spending without connecting my bank?**
Yes. Apps like Lumi and Finny use the iOS Shortcuts Wallet automation, which runs on your device and logs the transaction the instant you tap to pay. No bank login is involved, so your credentials never leave your phone.

**Does the Apple Pay automation capture online purchases too?**
No. The Wallet automation trigger fires for in-store tap-to-pay transactions, not for online or in-app Apple Pay checkouts. That is why a good tracker also offers quick manual capture — for example, Lumi adds Back Tap, voice, and AI receipt scanning to cover everything the tap automation misses.

**Is Apple Card required to track Apple Pay spending?**
No. Apple Card gives you nicer built-in summaries inside Wallet, but it only covers Apple Card purchases. To track every card you tap with Apple Pay, use a Shortcut-based app like Lumi or Finny, which works with any card you add to the automation.
