**The best budget app for a freelancer is the one that handles all three freelancer problems at once: income that changes every month, expenses that must survive a tax audit, and often clients who pay in different currencies.** Most budgeting apps are built for salaries. Here's what actually fits freelance money in 2026.

## What freelancers need that salaried people don't

**Variable-income planning.** A budget built on "monthly income" breaks when income is $6,200 one month and $1,800 the next. You need baseline budgeting plus a buffer — the system we describe in [how to budget with variable income](/blog/how-to-budget-with-variable-income).

**Audit-ready expense records.** Every deductible expense needs a date, amount, merchant, and category you can export at tax time — see [how to track expenses for taxes](/blog/how-to-track-expenses-for-taxes).

**Multi-currency reality.** A client pays in USD, subscriptions bill in EUR, you live in a third currency. Most US-centric apps simply don't model this; [how to track multiple currencies](/blog/how-to-track-multiple-currencies) explains what to look for.

## The picks, honestly

### Lumi — for freelancers who invoice internationally and hate bookkeeping

[Lumi](/) covers the freelancer triad without a bank login: log expenses by voice ("client lunch thirty-two dollars"), scan receipts in 2–3 seconds with amounts and merchants extracted automatically — including foreign-language receipts — and track everything across 150+ currencies converted to your home currency in real time. The AI month-end forecast is effectively a variable-income early-warning system: it projects your balance from your actual 3-month spend rate, so a thin month stops being a surprise. Data stays on your device — relevant if your "business account" is also your personal account and you'd rather not hand an aggregator both.

### YNAB — for freelancers whose problem is discipline, not records

YNAB's method genuinely shines with irregular income: you budget only money you already have, so a fat month funds the lean one automatically. The trade-offs: $109/yr, a real learning curve, weak multi-currency, and bank-sync-centric design. If the method appeals but the app doesn't, the principles work anywhere — see [zero-based budgeting](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting).

### Monarch — for freelancers who want the full dashboard

Strong reports and net-worth tracking make Monarch attractive once freelancing becomes a stable business with US accounts. Budgets remain salary-shaped, there's no multi-currency support, and bank linking is mandatory — fine for some, disqualifying for others.

### Goodbudget or a spreadsheet — the manual baseline

Both work if you have the discipline. Both die the way manual systems die: entry friction. A freelancer logging 60 deductible expenses a month through forms usually stops by March.

## The bottom line

International clients, mixed currencies, receipts to keep: Lumi. Discipline problem with US-only income: YNAB. Established business wanting a dashboard: Monarch. ## FAQ

### How should a freelancer budget with irregular income?

Budget from a baseline (your lowest realistic month), keep a 1–3 month income buffer, and pay yourself a fixed "salary" from it. Full system: [how to budget with variable income](/blog/how-to-budget-with-variable-income).

### Do freelancers need a separate business budget app?

Below roughly full accounting-software scale, one tracker with clean categories (business vs personal) plus export covers it. What matters is that every business expense is logged with merchant, date, and receipt — not which app holds it.

### What's the best way to track expenses in multiple currencies?

Use a tracker with per-transaction currencies and automatic conversion to one home currency — otherwise your monthly total is fiction. Lumi supports 150+ currencies with live rates on the paid plan (2 on free).
