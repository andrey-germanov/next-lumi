"Save more this year" is the most common financial goal there is, and also the one people abandon fastest — usually because the goal lives in your head instead of somewhere you look at daily. The apps in this category split into two very different approaches: **automated-transfer apps** that move money for you, and **tracking apps** that keep the goal visible and let you log progress yourself. Most people who actually reach a goal end up using one of each. Here's how the main options compare.

## The two categories

**Automated transfer apps** (Qapital, Digit, Acorns, Chime) connect to your bank and move small amounts of money based on rules — round-ups, spending patterns, or a schedule. You barely have to think about it, which is the whole appeal, but it requires handing over bank access and trusting an algorithm with your cash flow.

**Tracking and motivation apps** (Lumi, YNAB's goal feature) don't move money at all. You set a target, log contributions as you make them, and the app shows progress, milestones, and how much is left. No bank access required, but no automation either — you have to actually move the money yourself.

## Qapital — Rule-based automation

Qapital lets you build "if this, then save" rules: round up every purchase, save $5 every time you order coffee, save a fixed amount every payday. It's the most customizable of the automated apps and genuinely fun to set up.

The catch is the same as any bank-linked app: you're sharing account access, and the rules can move money you didn't quite mean to move if your spending pattern changes. Free tier is limited; most useful features sit behind a monthly subscription.

**Best for:** People who want savings to happen automatically and don't mind linking their bank to make it happen.

## Digit — Algorithmic "invisible" saving

Digit analyzes your income and spending pattern and quietly moves small, variable amounts into savings — the pitch is you won't even notice the money is gone. It's the most hands-off option on this list; there's no manual rule-setting.

It requires full bank access to work, is subscription-only (no meaningful free tier), and because the algorithm decides how much to save, your progress toward a specific goal is less predictable than a fixed transfer.

**Best for:** People who've tried manual saving and just want an algorithm to handle it without their input.

## Acorns — Round-ups that invest, not just save

Acorns rounds up your purchases to the nearest dollar and invests the difference in a diversified portfolio, rather than parking it in cash. It's less a "savings goal" app and more a micro-investing app that happens to use the same round-up mechanic as Qapital.

That distinction matters: invested money can go down as well as up, so it's a poor fit for a short-term goal like a vacation next spring. It's a reasonable fit for long-term goals where you can ride out volatility. Paid subscription required.

**Best for:** Long-term goals (5+ years out) where you're comfortable with market risk instead of guaranteed cash savings.

## Chime — Automatic savings baked into a bank account

Chime is a neobank (banking app) with round-up and automatic-savings features built in as part of the checking/savings account itself, not a separate goals product. If you're open to switching your primary bank account, it's a genuinely free way to get automated round-up savings without a subscription.

It's a bank account, not a dedicated goals app — there's no rich milestone tracking or multiple named goals with progress bars the way dedicated apps offer.

**Best for:** People willing to make Chime their everyday bank account in exchange for free automatic saving.

## YNAB — Goals as part of a full budget

YNAB's goal feature lets you assign a target and a deadline to any budget category, and it'll tell you how much to set aside per paycheck to hit it. It's tightly integrated with the rest of YNAB's zero-based budgeting system, which is either a strength (goals live inside your real budget, not a separate silo) or a barrier (you have to adopt the whole YNAB method to get it), depending on how you look at it.

No automated transfers — YNAB tracks money, it doesn't move it. Requires bank sync for the tracking to stay accurate, and the full subscription (~$109/year) to access any of it.

**Best for:** People already committed to YNAB's budgeting method who want goals tracked in the same place as everything else.

## Lumi — Goal tracking without linking your bank

Lumi's savings goals work like YNAB's in spirit — set a target, optionally a deadline, log contributions, watch a progress bar and milestones — but without requiring bank access at all. You log a contribution the same way you log an expense: manually, via Back Tap, or by voice ("moved $50 to Vietnam trip"). No algorithm decides how much to save; you stay in control, and nothing moves automatically.

That's a deliberate tradeoff. If you want money to move itself, an automated-transfer app will always do more of the work for you. What Lumi does instead is make the goal impossible to forget about — it sits next to your everyday spending and forecast, not in a separate app you only open once a month. For a lot of people, "highly visible target you're logging weekly" beats "invisible algorithm you never check," because visibility is what actually keeps a goal alive.

Multiple goals run in parallel are supported on the free plan at no cost, with no bank login and no subscription required to start.

**Best for:** Anyone who wants a clear, visible savings goal alongside their everyday spending — without connecting a bank account, and especially if they're already tracking expenses with Lumi.

## Which one should you actually use?

If you want money moved for you and don't mind linking your bank: **Qapital** for control and customization, **Digit** if you want it fully hands-off, **Chime** if you're open to switching banks for a free option, **Acorns** only for long-term, risk-tolerant goals.

If you'd rather keep full control, skip the bank link, and just need the goal to stay visible enough that you actually hit it: **Lumi** or **YNAB's goal feature** if you're already budgeting there.

The two approaches aren't mutually exclusive — plenty of people run an automated round-up app in the background *and* track the goal in Lumi so it stays visible day to day instead of being out of sight, out of mind.
