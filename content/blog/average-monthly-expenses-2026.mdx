**The average US household spends about $6,545 per month (roughly $78,500 a year, per the BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey), and a single-person household around $4,800 per month in 2026-adjusted terms.** Housing is the dominant line at roughly a third of all spending. Here's the full category breakdown — and, more usefully, how to tell whether *your* numbers are high, low, or just fine.

## Average monthly expenses by category

Based on the latest [BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cesan.nr0.htm) data (2024 survey, the most recent published), scaled to monthly figures:

| Category | Avg household / month | Share of spending |
|----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Housing | ~$2,210 | ~33% |
| Transportation | ~$1,100 | ~17% |
| Food (groceries + eating out) | ~$1,080 | ~16% |
| Insurance & pensions | ~$820 | ~13% |
| Healthcare | ~$510 | ~8% |
| Entertainment | ~$310 | ~5% |
| Apparel & services | ~$160 | ~2% |
| Everything else | ~$355 | ~6% |
| **Total** | **~$6,545** | 100% |

For a single person, the structure is similar but the total is lower — around **$4,800/month**, with housing taking an even larger share (30–36%), because rent doesn't split.

## Why averages mislead (and what to use instead)

Averages blend Manhattan rents with rural mortgages and single households with families of five. A more honest benchmark is *shares*, not dollars: housing under ~35% of take-home, food around 10–15%, transport under ~15%. That's exactly the logic of the [50/30/20 rule](/blog/what-is-the-50-30-20-rule) — needs, wants, savings as percentages that scale with your income. Run your own split in the [50/30/20 calculator](/tools/50-30-20-budget-calculator).

## How to find your real numbers

Most people can quote their rent but not their food or subscription totals — the categories where [overspending actually hides](/blog/how-to-stop-overspending). Getting your real baseline takes one month of complete tracking: every purchase, including the invisible ones ([Apple Pay taps](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending), [cash](/blog/how-to-track-cash-spending), [subscriptions](/blog/how-to-track-subscriptions)). The catch is that manual tracking usually dies before the month ends — which is why capture speed matters: voice logging ("groceries forty dollars"), automatic Apple Pay import, and receipt scanning keep the record complete without the ritual. ## What "normal" looks like at different incomes

Spending scales with income, but not linearly — housing and food dominate at lower incomes, while savings rate is what grows at higher ones. The single fastest-growing paycheck-to-paycheck group is $100k–200k households ([full statistics](/blog/budgeting-statistics-2026)), proof that income alone doesn't fix a budget: awareness plus a plan does. ## FAQ

### What are average monthly expenses for one person in 2026?

Roughly $4,800/month for a single-person urban household (BLS-based, inflation-adjusted), with housing at 30–36% of that. Your number can be far lower — the average includes high-cost metros.

### What is the average household budget per month?

About $6,545/month ($78,500/year) per the latest BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey — housing ~$2,210, transport ~$1,100, food ~$1,080 leading the categories.

### How much should I spend per category?

Use shares, not averages: housing under ~35% of take-home pay, food 10–15%, transport under ~15%, with 20% going to savings if possible. The [50/30/20 calculator](/tools/50-30-20-budget-calculator) turns your income into concrete numbers.

### How do I know if I overspend?

Track one complete month, then compare each category's share against the benchmarks above. The comparison only works if the record is complete — the usual gaps are contactless payments, cash, and subscriptions, which is what automatic capture solves.
