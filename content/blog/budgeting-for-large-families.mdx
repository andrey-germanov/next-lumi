**Large-family budgeting isn't small-family budgeting multiplied — the categories change rank (groceries become the #2 expense after housing), per-child costs fall as kids stack, and the volume of transactions breaks every manual tracking system.** Here's the playbook for households of five and up.

## The math that changes at five people

Good news first: kids benefit from economies of scale — hand-me-downs, shared rooms, bulk cooking mean the fourth child costs far less than the first. The bad news: the absolute numbers are still big, and they concentrate in food. A [family of five spends $1,200–1,950/month on groceries](/blog/grocery-budget-family-of-5); [a family of six, $1,550–2,550](/blog/grocery-budget-family-of-6) — USDA figures that make food the budget's second line and its most controllable one. Housing scales in steps (the 3-to-4-bedroom jump), vehicles in cliffs (the minivan-or-second-car moment), and healthcare in premiums that finally reward the family plan.

## The large-family system

1. **Budget groceries as infrastructure.** Weekly cap, bulk staples, [meal-planned around sales](/blog/how-much-to-spend-on-groceries) — at this scale a 15% grocery improvement is $200–350/month, more than most families' entire subscription audit.
2. **Zero-base the rest.** With this many moving parts, [every dollar needs a job](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting); "whatever's left" is always gone. [Sinking funds](/blog/what-is-a-sinking-fund) per predictable irregular: school-year startup, sports seasons, birthdays (there are many), car repairs.
3. **Make tracking survive the chaos.** Two adults, five stores, a dozen taps a day — the spreadsheet died years ago. Capture has to cost nothing: [say it](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing) ("Costco two eighty"), let [Apple Pay import itself](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending), scan the receipt. That's [Lumi](/)'s exact job, and both parents logging into a [shared picture](/blog/what-is-a-shared-budget) is what makes the numbers true.
4. **Protect the parents' finish line.** The large-family trap with the longest consequences: five college funds and no retirement. The [order of operations](/blog/how-to-budget-in-your-40s) doesn't change because the family is big — retirement first, college from surplus. Your kids get decades to repay loans; you get one shot at [the 50 checkpoint](/blog/how-much-savings-by-50).

## Benchmarks for big households

A [family of four baseline runs $6,000–9,000/month](/blog/family-of-4-monthly-budget) nationally; each additional child adds roughly $400–800 depending on age — meaning family-of-six totals of $7,000–10,500 outside expensive metros ([city breakdowns](/blog/cost-of-living-in-houston) matter more at this scale than any other). ## FAQ

**How much does a family of 5 or 6 need monthly?** Roughly $6,500–9,500 (five) and $7,000–10,500 (six) outside high-cost metros — housing and groceries dominate. See the [grocery-specific breakdowns](/blog/grocery-budget-family-of-5).

**What's the best budget method for a big family?** Zero-based with sinking funds, plus effortless shared tracking. Envelope logic works; [paper envelopes](/blog/what-is-the-envelope-method) don't survive seven schedules.

**Where do large families overspend most?** Food waste (10–20% of a big grocery spend), duplicate subscriptions across devices, and unplanned per-kid activity costs. The tracked month finds all three.
