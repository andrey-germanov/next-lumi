**Impulse spending** is unplanned buying that happens in the moment, driven by emotion, urgency, or convenience rather than a real need. It's the coffee you didn't plan for, the cart add-on at checkout, or the "treat yourself" purchase after a hard day. The decision takes seconds, but the cost lingers on your statement.

## How it works

Impulse spending usually follows a quick loop: a feeling or cue appears, a purchase promises instant relief, and you buy before the rational part of your brain weighs in. Say you're scrolling at night, you feel bored, and an ad shows a gadget for a limited-time price. The urgency ("only a few left") and the low friction (saved card, one-tap buy) combine to close the sale in under a minute.

Retailers design for this on purpose: checkout-line snacks, "customers also bought" rows, and countdown timers all shorten the gap between wanting and buying. The shorter that gap, the more impulse purchases slip through.

## Why it matters

A single impulse buy rarely breaks a budget. The problem is volume. Ten small unplanned purchases a week can quietly add up to more than a car payment by month's end, and because each one felt minor, none of them registered as a real decision.

Common mistakes that keep the habit going:

- **Shopping while emotional** — tired, stressed, or celebrating. Feelings drive the purchase, not need.
- **Saving payment details everywhere**, so buying takes one tap and no thought.
- **Not tracking small purchases**, which makes the total invisible until the statement lands.

The fix isn't willpower alone. It's adding friction and visibility: a short pause before buying, and a running record so the small stuff stops hiding.
