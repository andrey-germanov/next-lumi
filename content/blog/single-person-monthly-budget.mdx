**The average single person in the US spends roughly $3,700–4,800 per month, and structurally more of it goes to housing — 30–36% of income versus ~31% for couples — because rent is the one bill nobody splits with you.** Here are the category averages, a workable template, and the two places solo budgets actually leak.

## Average monthly expenses for one person

Based on BLS Consumer Expenditure data scaled to single-person households ([full methodology](/blog/average-monthly-expenses-2026)):

| Category | Monthly average | Share |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Housing (rent/mortgage + utilities) | $1,800–2,200 | 30–36% |
| Transportation | $600–760 | ~15% |
| Food (groceries + eating out) | $650–850 | ~16% |
| Healthcare & insurance | $450–550 | ~11% |
| Entertainment & personal | $300–450 | ~8% |
| Everything else | $350–500 | ~10% |
| **Total** | **~$3,700–4,800** | 100% |

Location swings this hard: the same solo life costs [$4,000–6,500 in NYC](/blog/cost-of-living-in-new-york-city) and [$2,100–3,000 in Houston](/blog/cost-of-living-in-houston).

## The solo premium — and its hidden discount

Living alone means paying 100% of rent, utilities, and streaming on one income — the "singles tax" is real, mostly in housing ([the living-alone guide](/blog/budgeting-when-living-alone) covers how to shrink it). But solo budgets hold a hidden advantage: one decision-maker. No negotiation, no partner's habits, instant course-correction. Solo budgeters who track their spending adjust faster than any household type — the entire budget is one conversation with yourself.

## A realistic template for one

Start from [50/30/20](/blog/what-is-the-50-30-20-rule) but expect needs to run 50–60% solo — that's structure, not failure. Non-negotiables: an [emergency fund](/tools/emergency-fund-calculator) sized honestly (no second income catches you — aim 6 months, not 3), automatic savings [before spending](/blog/what-is-pay-yourself-first), and [sinking funds](/blog/what-is-a-sinking-fund) for the irregulars you face alone (car, travel, medical).

## Where solo budgets leak

Two places, reliably. Food: cooking for one defaults to delivery — the average solo eater's restaurant/delivery line often matches their grocery line ([grocery benchmarks for one](/blog/how-much-to-spend-on-groceries): $250–400/month is workable). And frictionless small spending: nobody sees your taps, so nothing questions them — [the Apple Pay effect](/blog/does-apple-pay-make-you-spend-more) hits solo budgets hardest. The counter is a system that sees what a partner would: [Lumi](/) auto-imports the taps, takes [voice entries](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing) ("sushi twenty two"), and shows the month against the plan. One tracked month typically finds $200–400 of invisible solo spend.

## FAQ

**What's a good budget for a single person on $50k?** Take-home ~$3,400: aim $1,400–1,700 housing, $500–600 food, $550+ savings, rest to transport/fun. Tight in coastal cities — see the [city breakdowns](/blog/cost-of-living-in-chicago).

**How much should a single person save monthly?** 15–20% of take-home where possible; solo means your own safety net, so the [emergency fund](/blog/what-is-an-emergency-fund) comes before investing goals.

**Is living alone worth the cost?** That's a values question a budget can only price: typically $400–900/month over having a roommate. The [living-alone budget guide](/blog/budgeting-when-living-alone) does the math both ways.
