A family budget app has a harder job than a personal one: multiple adults spending in parallel, hundreds of transactions a month, kids entering the money picture, and a system that fails the moment one parent stops participating. The right choice depends on which of those problems hurts most — shared visibility, kids' financial training, or capture volume. Here's the honest comparison.

## The apps, honestly compared

### Monarch Money — the full-picture household

Monarch's strength is breadth: unlimited household members, linked accounts, investments, and shared goals in one dashboard. For families that want *everything* visible and don't mind bank linking or the price, it's the current category leader. The trade-offs: cost, and a data model built on granting a third party read access to every family account.

### YNAB — powerful if both parents commit

Zero-based budgeting fits family life well ([why](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting)), and YNAB Together covers five users. But YNAB demands buy-in measured in weeks of habit-building — brilliant for two committed budgeters, fragile when one parent is being dragged along. Worth it for method families; see [is YNAB worth it](/blog/is-ynab-worth-it-2026) for the full math.

### EveryDollar — the Ramsey path

Free manual version, clean zero-based design, natural fit for Baby Steps families. The premium tier (bank sync) is expensive for what it adds; the manual tier's weakness is the classic one — manual entry across a family's transaction volume is where tracking goes to die.

### FamZoo and Greenlight — the kids' money specialists

Neither is a household budgeting app; both are excellent at the kids' side — prepaid cards, allowances, chores, savings splits. Families often run one of these *alongside* a household tracker rather than instead of one. FamZoo is the frugal, education-first pick; Greenlight the slicker, feature-rich one.

### Lumi — capture that survives family chaos

[Lumi](/) attacks the problem the others assume away: whether spending actually gets recorded. No bank login — data stays on-device — and logging costs seconds: say ["groceries one forty"](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing), let [Apple Pay purchases import themselves](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending), scan the Costco receipt. Both parents feed one [shared picture](/blog/what-is-a-shared-budget) without anyone maintaining a spreadsheet, across multiple accounts and 150+ currencies. What it doesn't do: kids' debit cards or investment dashboards. For families whose last three apps died of non-participation — usually the [large-family, high-volume households](/blog/budgeting-for-large-families) — capture-first is the feature that matters.

## The bottom line

Want everything in one dashboard and accept bank linking: Monarch. Both parents methodical: YNAB. Ramsey followers: EveryDollar. Teaching kids: FamZoo or Greenlight alongside your tracker. Privacy-first and tired of apps that die when logging stops: Lumi. Whatever you pick, anchor it to real numbers — the [family-of-4 budget benchmarks](/blog/family-of-4-monthly-budget) and [grocery tiers](/blog/grocery-budget-family-of-4) tell you what normal looks like.
