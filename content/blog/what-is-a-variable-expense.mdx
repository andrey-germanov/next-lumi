A **variable expense** is a cost that changes from month to month depending on how much you use or buy. Groceries, gas, electricity, dining out, and phone data overages are all variable expenses. Unlike a fixed bill that stays the same, variable expenses rise and fall — which makes them harder to predict but easier to adjust.

## How it works

Variable expenses sit between the two extremes of a budget. Fixed expenses (rent, insurance) never move; variable expenses shift with your behavior and the seasons.

Imagine your grocery spending over three months:

- **January: $420**
- **February: $510**
- **March: $380**

That is a **$130 swing** on a single category. To budget for it, use an average rather than a single month. Add the three months (**$1,310**) and divide by three to get roughly **$437**. Budget slightly above the average — say **$450** — to absorb a high month without blowing the plan.

Utilities behave the same way. A heating bill might be $180 in winter and $60 in summer. Averaging across the year, or building a small buffer for peak months, keeps a predictable-but-irregular cost from feeling like a surprise.

## Why it matters

Variable expenses are where budgets are won or lost, for one simple reason: **they are the costs you can actually control.** You cannot easily change your rent this month, but you can change how often you order takeout.

Common mistakes:

- **Budgeting off one good month.** Base a category on your lowest month and every average month feels like overspending.
- **Ignoring the swings.** A category that averages fine can still cause an overdraft in its peak month if you have no buffer.
- **Confusing variable with discretionary.** Some variable expenses are needs (groceries, electricity); some are wants (dining out). Both move, but you cut them differently.

The fix is to track variable categories closely and review them weekly, so you catch a high month while there is still time to pull back.
