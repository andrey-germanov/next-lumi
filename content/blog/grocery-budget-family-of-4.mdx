**The USDA's 2026 food plans put a family of four's groceries at $993/month (Thrifty), ~$1,092 (Low-Cost), $1,347 (Moderate), and $1,627 (Liberal) — all meals cooked at home.** If your number sits inside that band, you're normal; the interesting question is which tier you're *choosing* versus drifting into. Here's the breakdown.

## The USDA benchmarks for a family of 4

| Plan | Monthly cost | Weekly |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Thrifty | $993 | ~$229 |
| Low-Cost | ~$1,092 | ~$253 |
| Moderate | $1,347 | ~$311 |
| Liberal | $1,627 | ~$376 |

Source: [USDA Food Plans](https://www.fna.usda.gov/research/cnpp/usda-food-plans/cost-food-monthly-reports), reference family of two adults (19–50) and two children (6–8, 9–11), updated monthly for inflation. Teens eat more — a family with two teenagers should read one tier higher. Larger families: [family of 5](/blog/grocery-budget-family-of-5), [family of 6](/blog/grocery-budget-family-of-6).

## What the tiers actually mean

The $634/month spread between Thrifty and Liberal buys the *same nutrition* — the difference is convenience foods, name brands, premium cuts, and food waste. That makes groceries the most controllable large line in a [family budget](/blog/family-of-4-monthly-budget): moving one tier down is a $250–350/month decision that requires zero sacrifice of nutrition, only of defaults. The [general grocery guide](/blog/how-much-to-spend-on-groceries) covers the mechanics; the short version: plan meals around sales, store brands (5–70% cheaper), bulk staples, and — the quiet one — waste, which averages 10–20% of family grocery spend.

## Finding your real number

Most families guess their grocery number $150–300 low, because "groceries" leaks across five stores, Target runs that are half food, and [tapped card payments](/blog/does-apple-pay-make-you-spend-more) nobody memorizes. One complete tracked month fixes the data: [Lumi](/) imports [Apple Pay purchases automatically](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending), takes [voice entries](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing) in the parking lot ("Costco two forty"), and scans receipts — so the month-end grocery total is true for the first time. Compare it to the table above, pick a target tier, and set it as a [spending limit](/blog/what-is-a-spending-limit).

## Cutting 15% without coupons

The moves ranked by return-per-effort: a weekly meal plan built from the sales flyer (10–15% alone), one bulk store trip monthly for staples, store brands for everything the kids don't notice, a leftovers night that actually happens, and a freezer inventory before shopping (kills duplicate buying — the #1 waste source). Skip extreme couponing; the hours don't pay at family scale.

## FAQ

**Is $1,200 a month normal for a family of 4?** Yes — between USDA Low-Cost and Moderate, right at the national norm. With teens, it reads as Low-Cost.

**What's a realistic weekly grocery budget for 4?** $230–310 for most families; under $230 takes deliberate Thrifty-plan discipline, over $375 is Liberal-tier by choice or by drift.

**Does eating out count?** Not in USDA numbers — they assume all meals at home. Track restaurants separately; it's usually where the "grocery overspend" actually lives.
