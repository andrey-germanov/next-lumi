A **spending limit** is a preset cap on how much you'll spend in a given category or time period, such as 300 dollars a month on dining out or 50 dollars a week on coffee. When you reach the limit, you stop or pause. Limits turn a vague intention to "spend less" into a concrete, trackable number.

## How it works

You set a limit by choosing a category, a period, and an amount, ideally based on your real income and goals rather than a guess. For example, using a 50/30/20 split, you might cap all "wants" at 30 percent of take-home pay, then break that into sub-limits: 200 for dining, 100 for shopping, 60 for entertainment. As purchases land, you track them against each cap and adjust behavior as you approach the line.

The most effective limits share three traits:

- **Specific** — tied to one category, not your whole life.
- **Realistic** — based on what you actually spend, then trimmed gradually.
- **Visible** — checked often, with an alert as you near the cap.

## Why it matters

Without limits, spending expands to fill whatever is in your account, and you only learn you overspent after the statement arrives. A limit moves that feedback earlier, giving you a chance to change course while it still matters. It also makes trade-offs obvious: if dining is near its cap mid-month, you can decide to cook rather than blow past it.

Common mistakes: setting limits so tight they feel like punishment (which leads to quitting), setting them once and never revisiting, or setting them without any way to see your running total. A limit with no tracking is just a wish. Pair each cap with a real-time count and an alert, and it becomes a guardrail you'll actually follow.
