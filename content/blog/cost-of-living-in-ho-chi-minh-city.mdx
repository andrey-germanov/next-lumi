**A single expat lives very comfortably in Ho Chi Minh City on $900–1,400 per month in 2026; frugal setups manage $650–900, and the full Thao Dien expat package runs $1,800–2,500.** Saigon is 20–40% cheaper than Bangkok or KL, driven by low rents and a street-food economy where $5 feeds two people. Here's the honest math.

## The two-speed city

HCMC runs two parallel economies more sharply than anywhere in the region: a Thao Dien villa-and-brunch circuit priced in dollars, and a local city where a full meal with beer costs $2.50. The same person's budget can differ by 3x depending on which economy is the default — rent is the anchor decision ($700–1,200 in Thao Dien vs $230–400 in Binh Thanh next door), and food follows the same split. Neither choice is wrong; not knowing which one you're making is.

## The invisible spending problem

Saigon's costs are dozens of tiny transactions: 25,000 VND coffees, 60,000 VND GrabBike rides, 40,000 VND bánh mì — cash and QR payments too small to remember and too frequent to ignore. Five-figure dong amounts don't register as money ($1 ≈ 25,000 VND), which is exactly how $900 months become $1,400. A two-second voice log ("phở fifty thousand dong") at the stall beats month-end archaeology — [tracking cash](/blog/how-to-track-cash-spending) is where Saigon budgets get honest.

## FAQ

### How much money do you need to live in Ho Chi Minh City?

$900–1,400/month for a very comfortable single life in 2026; $650–900 frugal; $1,800–2,500 for the full expat-district package with Western food and mid-tier insurance.

### Is Vietnam cheaper than Thailand?

HCMC runs 20–40% cheaper than Bangkok overall, driven by rent and daily costs. Bangkok wins on infrastructure and transit; Saigon wins on price and food value.

### How much is rent in Saigon for foreigners?

$500–850 for a modern one-bedroom in expat-friendly areas (Thao Dien, central D1); $230–400 in local central districts like Binh Thanh; under $200 further out.

### What do expats forget to budget in HCMC?

Visa runs or business-visa costs, health insurance ($80–150), AC-driven electricity spikes, and the slow drift from local to Western food defaults — the last one alone can double a food budget. A [category limit](/blog/what-is-a-spending-limit) on Western dining keeps it visible.
