**Your 30s are the squeeze: income finally grows, but mortgages, childcare, and a decade of accumulated [lifestyle creep](/blog/what-is-lifestyle-creep) grow faster.** It's the decade most budgets break — and the decade the Fed's own data shows savings stagnating. The fix isn't discipline; it's structure. Here's the playbook.

## What changes at 30

The 20s budget had one stakeholder and mostly variable costs. The 30s version carries fixed obligations — mortgage or family-sized rent, insurance, childcare that rivals rent — often a partner with their own money habits, and stakes: the [2x-salary-by-35 checkpoint](/blog/how-much-savings-by-35) arrives mid-decade. Fixed costs mean less slack; less slack means guessing stops working.

## The 30s budget, step by step

1. **Re-derive your numbers from scratch.** The mental model from your 20s is obsolete. One complete month of tracking — made survivable by [voice logging](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing) and [automatic Apple Pay import](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending) in [Lumi](/) — replaces guesses with data. Households in their 30s typically find $200–500/month they couldn't name ([the statistics agree](/blog/budgeting-statistics-2026)).
2. **Upgrade the method to match the complexity.** 50/30/20 was fine solo; with fixed costs dominating, [zero-based budgeting](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting) — every dollar assigned a job — fits better. Irregular costs (car repairs, kid stuff, holidays) get [sinking funds](/blog/what-is-a-sinking-fund) instead of panic.
3. **Get the partner system right.** Most 30s money stress is coordination, not math: who pays what, who sees what. Pick a model deliberately — [joint, separate, or hybrid](/blog/how-to-budget-as-a-couple-separate-accounts), with a [shared budget](/blog/what-is-a-shared-budget) for common categories and the [split calculator](/tools/couple-expense-split-calculator) for fairness.
4. **Protect the savings rate from the mortgage.** The classic 30s error: house first, savings from what's left — which is nothing. Automate retirement and emergency contributions *before* upgrading the lifestyle; the [emergency fund](/tools/emergency-fund-calculator) target is now 3–6 months of much bigger expenses.
5. **Keep the raise rule from your 20s.** Half of every raise to savings rate ([the 20s playbook](/blog/how-to-budget-in-your-20s)) matters *more* now — it's what closes the gap on [2x by 35](/blog/how-much-savings-by-35) and sets up [3x by 40](/blog/how-much-savings-by-40).

## The three classic 30s traps

House-poor by choice (needs above 60% of take-home leaves no system that works); childcare treated as temporary while it quietly cancels a savings rate for five years; and retirement paused "until things calm down" — the decade's compounding is too valuable to pause ([run the cost of waiting](/tools/compound-interest-calculator)).

## FAQ

**How much should I have saved in my 30s?** The benchmarks: [1x salary at 30](/blog/how-much-savings-by-30), [2x at 35](/blog/how-much-savings-by-35), 3x entering 40. The median household is far behind — the playbook above is the catch-up.

**50/30/20 doesn't fit my 30s budget — is that normal?** Common. With childcare and a mortgage, needs often exceed 50%. Use zero-based budgeting and protect the savings line first; the ratios recover when childcare ends.

**Biggest single lever in a 30s budget?** The audit. A decade of subscriptions, insurance never re-shopped, and creep — [the leak hunt](/blog/how-to-find-forgotten-subscriptions) funds most catch-up plans without touching quality of life.
