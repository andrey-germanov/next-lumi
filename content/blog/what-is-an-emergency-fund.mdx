An **emergency fund** is money you set aside specifically to cover unexpected, urgent expenses — a car repair, a medical bill, or a sudden loss of income — without reaching for a credit card or a loan. It is not for planned purchases or vacations. It exists for one job: to keep a surprise from turning into a crisis.

## How it works

The common guideline is to save **three to six months of essential expenses**. Essential means the bare minimum to keep your life running: rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, and minimum debt payments — not dining out or subscriptions.

Say your essential monthly costs are **$2,500**. A three-month fund is **$7,500**; a six-month fund is **$15,000**. That can feel enormous, so start smaller. A **$1,000 starter fund** covers most common emergencies and is a realistic first milestone. From there, saving **$200 a month** gets you to a full three-month cushion in roughly two and a half years — faster if you automate it.

Keep the money somewhere separate but reachable: a high-yield savings account works well. It should be liquid enough to access within a day or two, but not so convenient that you dip into it for everyday spending.

## Why it matters

A large share of people have no cushion at all — surveys routinely find that many households could not cover a surprise $400 or $1,000 expense from savings. Without a fund, a single unexpected bill becomes credit-card debt, and that debt compounds at rates that can top 20% a year.

The most common mistakes:

- **Treating it as "leftover" savings.** Whatever is left at month-end is usually nothing. Pay yourself first instead.
- **Aiming for the full six months on day one.** The size is intimidating and people quit. Hit $1,000 first, then build.
- **Mixing it with a spending account.** If it sits next to your grocery money, it disappears into grocery money.
- **Not refilling it.** After you use it, rebuilding it becomes the next goal.
