A **fixed expense** is a cost that stays the same amount every month, regardless of how you behave. Rent or mortgage, car payments, insurance premiums, and most subscriptions are fixed expenses. Because they are predictable, they form the stable foundation of a budget — the bills you can plan around with confidence.

## How it works

Fixed expenses are the first thing you list when building a budget, because they are the easiest to total: the number does not change from month to month.

A typical fixed-expense list might look like:

- **Rent: $1,400**
- **Car payment: $320**
- **Auto and renters insurance: $150**
- **Phone plan: $45**
- **Streaming and app subscriptions: $40**

That is **$1,955** committed before you buy a single thing. On a take-home income of **$4,000**, fixed expenses already claim nearly **49%** of your money. Whatever is left — about $2,045 — is what you have for variable costs, discretionary wants, and savings.

Knowing this number is powerful. It tells you exactly how much of your income is spoken for the moment you get paid, and it sets a ceiling on how much you can realistically save without renegotiating a big commitment.

## Why it matters

Fixed expenses feel safe because they are predictable, but that predictability is a double-edged sword. A too-high fixed cost quietly limits every other choice you make, month after month.

Common mistakes:

- **Assuming fixed means unchangeable.** Fixed means steady, not permanent. You can shop insurance, refinance a loan, or downgrade a plan.
- **Letting subscriptions pile up.** Each one is small and fixed, so it hides. Ten $10 subscriptions is $1,200 a year on autopilot.
- **Committing too much to fixed costs.** If housing and payments eat 60% or more of income, there is little room to absorb a bad month or build savings.

Because fixed expenses recur automatically, the biggest win is a periodic audit: once or twice a year, challenge every fixed line to justify itself.
