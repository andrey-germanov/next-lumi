YNAB (You Need A Budget) is one of the most respected budgeting apps ever built. Its zero-based method — give every dollar a job before you spend it — has genuinely changed how a lot of people handle money. So if you're searching for YNAB alternatives, it's usually not because YNAB is bad. It's because something about the fit is off.

Maybe it's the price: YNAB now costs **$14.99 per month, or $109 per year** ([YNAB pricing](https://www.ynab.com/pricing)). Maybe it's the learning curve — zero-based budgeting is powerful but demanding, and not everyone wants to assign every dollar a category. Or maybe it's the bank connection: YNAB works best when you link your accounts, and plenty of people would rather not hand a third party their banking logins.

If any of that sounds like you, this guide walks through the best YNAB alternatives in 2026 — with honest pricing, who each one is actually for, and where each falls short.

## Why switch from YNAB

Three reasons come up again and again:

- **Price.** At $109 a year with no free tier beyond a 34-day trial, YNAB is one of the pricier budgeting apps. If you're budgeting precisely because money is tight, a recurring $109 subscription can feel like a contradiction.
- **The learning curve.** Zero-based budgeting works, but it asks a lot: you assign every dollar to a category, reconcile regularly, and cover overspending by moving money between envelopes. Some people love that discipline. Others bounce off it within a month.
- **Bank sync.** YNAB is built around linking your bank accounts for automatic imports. That's convenient, but it means your financial data flows through a third-party aggregator — a dealbreaker if you value privacy or simply don't want another company holding your credentials.

None of these make YNAB a bad app. They just mean it isn't the right tool for everyone. Here are the alternatives worth comparing.

### Lumi — best for effortless, private, no-bank tracking

**Best for:** people who want to actually track spending without linking a bank, learning a system, or typing much at all.

**Pricing:** free plan, with Premium at **$4.99/month**.

Most budgeting apps fail for the same boring reason: logging is annoying, so you stop doing it. [Lumi](/) attacks that problem directly. On iPhone you can log a purchase in about two seconds using an iOS Back Tap gesture or straight from Apple Pay — no opening the app and tapping through menus. You can also scan a receipt with AI, or just say what you spent using voice entry.

Crucially, Lumi uses **no bank login**. Nothing is linked, nothing is aggregated — you log what you spend, and your data stays yours. It supports **150+ currencies** (great for travelers and anyone earning or spending across borders) and includes an AI-powered forecast that projects where your spending is heading.

**Pros:**

- Roughly two-second logging via Back Tap or Apple Pay
- AI receipt scanning and voice entry
- Privacy-first: no bank connection required
- 150+ currencies and AI spending forecasts
- Free plan; affordable Premium

**Cons:**

- iOS only right now
- Not a strict zero-based envelope system like YNAB — if you specifically want to assign every dollar to an envelope and reconcile against linked accounts, YNAB or Actual Budget will fit better
- Manual logging (made fast) rather than automatic bank imports

If your real problem is that you never stick with tracking, Lumi is the pick. If your problem is that you want rigorous envelope math, keep reading.

### YNAB — best for committed zero-based budgeters

**Best for:** people who want a proven zero-based system and will do the work.

**Pricing:** **$14.99/month or $109/year** ([source](https://www.ynab.com/pricing)); a full year is free with a valid .edu email, plus a 34-day trial for everyone.

Included here for fairness, because for the right person YNAB is still excellent. Its four rules and give-every-dollar-a-job method build real awareness and get people out of paycheck-to-paycheck cycles. The mobile and web apps are polished, and the community and educational content are among the best in the category. The tradeoffs are the ones that send people looking: the price, the effort, and the reliance on bank connections. If those don't bother you, YNAB may be worth keeping.

### Copilot — best for Apple users who want automation

**Best for:** iPhone, iPad, and Mac users who want automatic tracking with a clean design.

**Pricing:** about **$95/year or $13/month** ([Finny review](https://getfinny.app/blog/copilot-money-review-2026)).

Copilot is an Apple-ecosystem favorite with strong AI-driven transaction categorization and a genuinely beautiful interface. It added a web app in late 2025, but there's still no Android version. It leans on bank syncing for automation, so it solves the "typing is annoying" problem the opposite way Lumi does — by connecting your accounts. If you're all-in on Apple and comfortable linking banks, it's one of the nicer options.

### Monarch — best for households and net-worth tracking

**Best for:** couples and families who want collaborative budgeting plus investment and net-worth tracking.

**Pricing:** Monarch Core from **$99.99/year (about $8.33/month) or $14.99/month**; a Monarch Plus tier for power users runs $199/year ([Finny](https://getfinny.app/blog/monarch-vs-ynab-vs-simplifi-2026)).

Monarch has become the go-to for households since it works across iOS, Android, and web, and handles shared budgets, investments, and net worth in one place. It's flexible rather than strictly zero-based, which some ex-YNAB users find more forgiving. It's priced similarly to YNAB and also depends on bank connections, so it's a switch of philosophy more than a cost saving.

### Simplifi by Quicken — best for low-cost automated spending plans

**Best for:** people who want automated tracking and a spending plan without a premium price.

**Pricing:** **$71.88/year** standard, frequently discounted to around $47.88 for the first year ([CostBench](https://costbench.com/software/personal-finance/simplifi/)).

Simplifi is one of the better-value automated apps. Instead of strict envelopes, it builds a real-time spending plan that shows what's left to spend after bills and goals. It syncs with banks and covers iOS, Android, and web. If you like the automation of YNAB but want a lower price and a lighter method, Simplifi is a strong candidate.

### Goodbudget — best for digital envelope budgeters

**Best for:** couples who want the classic envelope method without linking banks.

**Pricing:** **free** plan (20 envelopes, 1 account, 2 devices), or **Premium at $80/year** ([FinCompareLab](https://www.fincomparelab.com/guides/goodbudget-pricing/)).

Goodbudget is a manual, envelope-based system — closest in spirit to the [envelope method](/blog/what-is-the-envelope-method) that inspired zero-based budgeting. The free tier is genuinely usable for a simple setup, and because it's manual by default, it appeals to privacy-minded budgeters. Syncing across a couple's phones makes it a favorite for shared budgets. The catch: entry is manual and the free plan's envelope limit is tight.

### EveryDollar — best for simple zero-based budgeting on a plan

**Best for:** people who want straightforward zero-based budgeting, especially Ramsey-plan followers.

**Pricing:** **free** for manual budgeting, or **Premium at $79.99/year ($6.67/month)**; month-to-month is a steep $17.99 ([CostBench](https://costbench.com/software/personal-finance/everydollar/)).

EveryDollar delivers zero-based budgeting in a simpler, more guided package than YNAB. The free version is manual-entry only; bank sync is a paid feature. If you want the give-every-dollar-a-job discipline of YNAB with a gentler on-ramp — and don't mind manual entry on the free tier — it's worth a look. Avoid the monthly plan; it's pricey.

### Actual Budget — best for technical, privacy-first DIY users

**Best for:** people who want zero-based envelope budgeting they fully control.

**Pricing:** **free and open source** if you self-host; an official cloud-hosted option runs **$7.95/month** ([Actual Budget](https://actualbudget.org/)).

Actual is a local-first, open-source app explicitly inspired by YNAB's envelope method — but with no subscription and no telemetry if you self-host. Your data can stay on your own machine or server, with optional end-to-end encryption. It's the most private way to get true zero-based budgeting. The tradeoff is setup: self-hosting takes technical know-how, and bank imports rely on separate services. For the right user, it's the closest philosophical match to YNAB at essentially no software cost.

### PocketGuard — best for seeing what's safe to spend

**Best for:** people who mainly want a clear "how much can I spend right now" number.

**Pricing:** **$74.99/year or $12.99/month**; the old free tier has been discontinued ([pricing](https://pocketguard.com/pricing/)).

PocketGuard's signature feature is its "In My Pocket" figure — after bills, goals, and essentials, here's what's actually spendable. It syncs with banks and works on iOS, Android, and web. It's less about detailed budgeting and more about guardrails against overspending, which pairs well with our guide on [how to stop overspending](/blog/how-to-stop-overspending). Note that the free version is gone, so it's a paid app now.

## How to choose

Start by naming the reason you're leaving YNAB, because it points straight to the answer:

- **You want the same method for less (or free).** Look at **Actual Budget** (free if you self-host) or **EveryDollar** (free manual zero-based). Both keep the give-every-dollar-a-job structure.
- **You don't want to link a bank.** **Lumi**, **Goodbudget**, and **Actual Budget** all work without bank connections. Lumi is the most effortless; Goodbudget and Actual are the most envelope-strict.
- **You want less effort, not more discipline.** **Lumi** for fast private logging, **Simplifi** or **Copilot** for hands-off automation via bank sync.
- **You're budgeting as a household.** **Monarch** for cross-platform net-worth and shared budgeting; **Goodbudget** for a simpler shared-envelope approach.
- **You just want to know what's safe to spend today.** **PocketGuard**.

If you're not even sure which budgeting method fits you, our guides on [how to budget](/blog/how-to-budget) and [zero-based budgeting](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting) are a good starting point — and if the real issue is consistency, [how to track spending that actually sticks](/blog/how-to-track-spending-that-actually-sticks) is the one to read.

## FAQ

**What is the closest YNAB alternative to the real thing?**

For the zero-based envelope method specifically, **Actual Budget** is the closest match — it was directly inspired by YNAB, is open source, and costs nothing if you self-host. **EveryDollar** is the closest simple, guided version. If your goal is just to actually track spending consistently rather than run strict envelopes, **Lumi** is the easiest to stick with.

**Is there a good free YNAB alternative?**

Yes. **Actual Budget** (self-hosted), **Goodbudget**, and **EveryDollar** all have free tiers, and **Lumi** offers a free plan with fast, private logging. Free tiers usually mean manual entry rather than automatic bank imports — which many people actually prefer for privacy.

**Which YNAB alternative works without linking my bank?**

**Lumi**, **Goodbudget**, and **Actual Budget** can all run without bank connections. Lumi is designed around fast manual logging (Back Tap, Apple Pay, receipt scan, voice) so skipping bank sync doesn't mean tedious data entry — you keep full privacy without giving up on tracking.
