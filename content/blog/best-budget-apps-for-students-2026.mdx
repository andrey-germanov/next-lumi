## The picks, honestly

### Lumi — for students who won't do data entry

Student spending is many small purchases — exactly the pattern manual forms can't survive. [Lumi](/)'s free plan covers the whole capture layer: say ["coffee three fifty"](/blog/how-to-log-expenses-without-typing) between classes, let [Apple Pay taps import themselves](/blog/how-to-track-apple-pay-spending), scan the occasional receipt. Two free budgets fit the student reality (food + everything else), and no bank login means no credentials shared — data stays on your phone. iOS only; that's the honest limitation.

### Goodbudget — for envelope-method believers

The digital [envelope method](/blog/what-is-the-envelope-method) with a workable free tier and cross-platform sync. Everything is manual entry, which is both the discipline and the failure mode — see how [manual habits collapse](/blog/how-to-track-spending-that-actually-sticks) before committing.

### PocketGuard — for the "in my pocket" number

The one automatic-sync option here with a usable free tier: it shows what's safe to spend after bills. The trade: bank login required, and the free version is deliberately limited.

### EveryDollar — for zero-based structure, free

Dave Ramsey's app; the free manual version teaches [zero-based budgeting](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting) properly. Bank sync is paywalled at ~$80/yr, which puts its paid tier out of the student conversation.

### Bread or Fleur — for motivation by design

[Bread](/blog/lumi-vs-bread) gamifies staying on budget (free core, ~$5 pro); [Fleur](/blog/lumi-vs-fleur) makes manual logging genuinely pleasant with a one-time purchase instead of a subscription. Both are honest, student-priced products — both live or die on your willingness to type entries.

## The student budget itself, in three lines

Income (loans, part-time work, parental support) minus fixed costs (rent, phone, transit pass) equals your weekly spendable — divide by weeks, not months, because student cash flow is weekly. Cap the two leakiest categories ([food delivery and impulse buys](/blog/what-is-impulse-spending)) with [spending limits](/blog/what-is-a-spending-limit), and put anything left toward a [starter emergency fund](/blog/how-to-save-money-on-a-low-income). The [50/30/20 calculator](/tools/50-30-20-budget-calculator) gives you the split in ten seconds.

## FAQ

### What's the best completely free budget app for students?

Depends on the failure mode you're avoiding. Won't type entries: Lumi's free tier (voice + auto-import). Want envelope discipline: Goodbudget. Want a "safe to spend" number and accept bank linking: PocketGuard.

### Is YNAB worth it for students?

Usually not at $109/yr — the method is excellent but the price and weekly-reconciling workload fit a later life stage. The [zero-based method itself is free](/blog/what-is-zero-based-budgeting); EveryDollar's free tier or any tracker's category budgets teach the same skill.

### How should students split shared expenses?

Track your own spending privately and settle up monthly — the [couple expense split calculator](/tools/couple-expense-split-calculator) works just as well for roommates as for couples.

### Do students need bank sync?

No — and at this life stage it's often impossible anyway (shared family accounts, banks without aggregator support). Voice logging and Apple Pay auto-import capture the same data without credentials.
